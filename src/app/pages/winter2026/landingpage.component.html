ECS 88 - Winter 2026
Ethics in an Age of Technology

This course examines moral reasoning, ethical theories, and their application to contemporary issues in technology. We will explore fundamental questions about what it means to make ethical decisions in an era of rapid innovation.
[Previous Offerings: Spring 2025]

Schedule & Assigned Readings

We will cover 10 chapters from the main textbook (Gordon Graham, Theories of Ethics, 1st Edition), one chapter per week, along with its supplementary chapter readings as indicated below. Additionally, selected brief complementary sections are assigned from "The Problems of Consensus: An Ethical Inquiry into Democratic and Decentralized Principles" (referred to as Consensus), a philosophical and theological investigation that begins with a fundamental question: "What does reaching agreement entail?" or "Why Vote?"

Grading Scheme

Below is the class policy and breakdown of how your final grade will be determined.

Class Policy In this class, we adhere to the UC Davis Code of Academic Conduct. We have a zero-tolerance policy regarding the use of AI (e.g., ChatGPT) and plagiarism during quizzes and term papers. Any violation will result in a grade of zero and will be reported to the Office of Student Support and Judicial Affairs. You may use AI tools to correct spelling or grammar mistakes; however, you must not ask AI tools to write for you or submit your writing to AI for polishing or rewriting. It is your responsibility to check with the instructor to confirm that your use of AI is allowed.

Team Work A fact of life is that when working in teams, whether in school or in society, conflicts and ethical issues can arise. It is essential to learn how to communicate and collaborate effectively while being receptive, open, and kind to one another. With kindness and reflection, we should aim to resolve all conflicts. It is the group's responsibility to manage its internal affairs, involving the instructor only when absolutely necessary. However, keep in mind that group restructuring will only be permitted in rare exceptional circumstances. Once a group is formed, we must learn to work together harmoniously for at least 10 weeks.

Additionally, every group member must contribute fairly to projects, whether for weekly presentations or term papers. Limited or no contribution will result in a grade of 0. Your grade will reflect the degree of your contributions. It is the team's responsibility to proactively report any unfair distribution of work.

Assignment Weight Details
Regular Attendance / Active Participation Growth You are expected to attend every lecture while engaging in thoughtful and insightful debate. Expressing concerns is healthy, critically analyzing information is healthy, and exercising the freedom of speech is your right; however, a lack of respect and civility is not acceptable and has no place in academic institutions and noble society.
Pop-up Quizzes (Individual assignment) 25% You will have at most one quiz each week, and the lowest score will be dropped. Quizzes will not be announced in advance, and there will be no opportunity to retake any quiz. If you miss a quiz, you will receive a score of 0. However, if you miss a quiz for valid reasons and can provide proper documentation (such as for illness or an emergency), you will not be penalized, and the missed quiz will not count toward your final grade.
Weekly Presentations (Team of 5) 20% Starting from Week 2 and continuing through Week 9, each team will present topics related to that week's readings and their connection to broader questions in applied ethics, such as Bioethics, Business Ethics, Environmental Ethics, Technology Ethics, Social Ethics, and AI Ethics, e.g., applied ethics reading by Prof. Rogaway and Prof. Koehl. Presentations are designed to be concise, no longer than 20 to 25 minutes, with time for lively discussion and debate to emerge. For each presentation, slides/Winter 2026 are required. Each team is expected to present 4-6 times and no more than one presentation per week. There will be one presentation during each lecture and discussion session. There will be three presentations per week.
Term Paper (Team of 5) 35% The paper must analyze one or more ethical theories applied to a contemporary technological issue in approximately 2500 words. The analysis must be based on a book; preferably, choose a book based on one of the topics covered in "The Problems of Consensus: An Ethical Inquiry into Democratic and Decentralized Principles" or suggestions by Prof. Rogaway. Each team must obtain approval for its chosen book from the instructor. Teams should email the instructor by no later than February 4, explaining their choice of book. Final term paper is due on March 12, 2026. Late submissions will incur a 10% penalty for each day they are late; papers submitted more than two days late will not be accepted.
Term Paper Presentations (Team of 5) 20% The final presentation will occur in the last week of class (Week 10), covering the term paper and reflections on seminar takeaways. slides/Winter 2026 are required.
